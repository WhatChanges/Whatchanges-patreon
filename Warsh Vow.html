Three Misses

What the playbook didn't see coming

Three forces broke the cool-CPI-means-rally thesis. Take them one at a time.

01 · The Warsh Vow

The Fed chair refused to play along

On July 16 — two days after the cool CPI print — Fed Chair Kevin Warsh spoke to Congress. The market expected him to acknowledge the cooling inflation and signal a September cut. He did the opposite.

"I will disappoint anyone who thinks I will deviate from our 2% inflation target."

He pledged price stability. He refused to hint at a July rate decision. He committed to the hawkish stance despite the cool CPI. This was the moment the playbook broke.

The playbook assumed Warsh's hike narrative was dead after the weak June NFP (57,000 jobs). It assumed cool CPI would confirm the death. It assumed the market would price in September cuts. Warsh refused to play along — and the market believed him. The dollar stayed strong. Gold and silver stayed weak.

The structural shift

This is more than one speech. For 18 months the market has operated under the assumption that weak data means Fed cuts. That assumption is now broken. Warsh is signaling he'll accept labor-market weakness in pursuit of 2% inflation — even a recession, if that's what it takes. This is the Volcker playbook: Paul Volcker raised rates into a recession in 1979–1982 to break inflation. Warsh appears to be reading from the same script.

If Warsh is serious

Weak NFP no longer means "Fed cuts." Cool CPI no longer means "Fed cuts." A recession no longer means "Fed cuts." The only thing that gets Warsh to cut is inflation hitting 2% on a sustained basis — and 3.5% isn't 2%. The "Fed pivot" thesis, the bull case for gold since 2023, isn't paused. It's dead.

02 · The Iran Escalation Acceleration

From fragile ceasefire to active conflict

The second miss: Iran escalation accelerated dramatically, faster than the playbook's war-premium assumptions could absorb.

This is no longer a fragile ceasefire with sporadic strikes. This is active, escalating military conflict. Oil prices have spiked — WTI at one-month highs, Brent rising, and the Strait of Hormuz, which carries roughly a fifth of global oil flow, effectively closed.

The trap

Higher oil eventually means higher inflation. Warsh has vowed to fight inflation. So higher oil means Warsh stays hawkish longer, the dollar stays strong, and precious metals stay weak. The war premium that should help gold is being overwhelmed by the inflation-fear channel that hurts it.

03 · The Oil Lag Illusion

Cool CPI wasn't the end of the story

The CPI preview post noted that oil feeds into CPI with a two-to-four week lag — June's report only captured the beginning of the oil effect, not the full spike. That was correct. What got missed was the implication.

June CPI printed 3.5%, cool, and gold briefly spiked above $4,100 on July 14. But within 24 hours the market realized: June was cool because the oil spike hasn't hit yet. July CPI (released August 12) is expected hot. August CPI (released September 11) is expected hotter still.

The market is now pricing in hot inflation for July and August, even though June was cool. That repricing is why gold reversed and broke through $4,000.

The Honest Take

This is the Volcker moment

The playbook was wrong because it assumed the Fed would respond to data. Warsh is not responding to data — he's responding to his own framework, and that framework says 2% inflation, no matter what.

Volcker broke the back of inflation in 1979–1982 by causing a recession. Warsh appears willing to do the same. If he succeeds, gold's structural bid stays intact — PBOC and Tether keep buying — but the short-term path is down, not up.

The bull case for gold is now a 2027–2028 story, not a 2026 story. The bear case — the 2011 analog, drift lower for years — is a real risk for the next 6–12 months.
